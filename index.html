About the Lab

Planetary surface science across scales

We study how planetary surfaces evolve, from microscopic environmental interactions to regional-scale landforms and geologic histories.

The Planetary Geology and Surface Simulation Laboratory, led by Dr. Trishit Ruj at the Institute for Planetary Materials, Okayama University, focuses on the exploration and analysis of planetary surfaces, particularly Mars, to understand their geological history, environmental change, and potential habitability.

Our work integrates planetary geology, remote sensing, geomorphology, geophysics, and controlled laboratory experiments. We use custom-built Martian environmental simulation facilities and field analog observations to connect surface processes on Earth with those on planetary bodies.

By combining experiments, mapping, and interpretation, we aim to advance planetary science in a way that is both process-driven and mission-relevant.

Experimental simulation

Controlled chamber studies for present-day Martian near-surface processes and material responses.

Field analog studies

Earth environments used to test and refine interpretations of planetary geomorphology and sedimentary processes.

Remote sensing and mapping

Orbital observations, geomorphic analysis, and image-based investigation across multiple planetary datasets.

Research

Current themes and past contributions

Our research ranges from modern Martian surface activity to ancient environmental transitions recorded in planetary landforms.

Current Research

Periglacial Processes on Mars

We examine Martian polygonal terrain and scalloped depressions to infer ice-related periglacial activity and seasonal freeze-thaw cycles.

Brine Stability Under Martian Conditions

We simulate present-day brine dynamics using environmental chambers to understand metastable liquid water occurrence in cold Martian regions.

Delta Morphodynamics and Ocean Regression

Through tank experiments and remote sensing, we reconstruct paleolake deltas and interpret large-scale regression signatures on ancient Mars.

Lunar Tectonics and Crustal Stresses

We analyze tectonic structures in polar and farside regions of the Moon to estimate crustal stress states and seismic hazard for future missions.

AI and Machine Learning in Planetary Mapping

We apply deep learning models to classify geomorphic features on Mars using high-resolution topography and hyperspectral datasets.

Past Research

Martian Thermal Evolution and Tectonics

We explored early lithospheric deformation in Mars' southern highlands, identifying crustal extension and compressional tectonics as indicators of ancient dynamic regimes.

Reference: Ruj et al., 2019 – Geoscience Frontiers

Wrinkle Ridge–Related Volcanism

Our research identified young volcanic features associated with wrinkle ridges on the Moon and Mars, challenging the assumption that compressional zones lack volcanism.

References: Ruj & Kawai, 2021 – Icarus, Zhang et al., 2023 – Nature Communications

Lunar Landing Site Hazard Assessment

We assessed lunar landing safety by modeling crustal stresses, analyzing slope instability, and identifying seismic hazards near targeted regions such as Chandrayaan-3's site.

References: Ruj et al., 2022 – Icarus, Sinha et al., 2023 – Icarus
